AIA Triangle Barkitecture Competition · Cline Design Associates · 2026
Competition Overview

AIA Triangle Barkitecture is an annual design-build competition for a dog park pavilion. Proceeds benefit local animal rescues. The deliverable is a single cast-concrete module at brick scale (6–12 in.) from which the full pavilion wall or canopy is assembled. Module count, layout, and overall form are design decisions downstream of the module geometry itself.

Designer: Cline Design Associates, Raleigh NC. The competition awards Best in Show, Greenest Paw, Craziest Cat, Fat Cat, People's Choice, and Graphic Bark.

Three Concept Territories

Three territories were identified at the outset, each sharing the same deep structural principle: simple local rules generating complex emergent global form.

Territory 1 — Motion
Dog tail wagging kinematics as generative form logic. Primary impulse for this phase. Data-derived from Ren et al. (2022): 19,937 wag cycles across 10 dogs.
Territory 2 — Light
Dappled light / komorebi / cherry blossom aperture geometry. Available as secondary layer. Not the driving form generator at this stage.
Territory 3 — Biology
Fur-flow directional texture via Gray-Scott reaction-diffusion. Available as secondary layer. Not the driving form generator at this stage.
Core Design Argument
The module is a frozen swept volume — the spatial record of a single tail wag cycle, cast in concrete. The formal logic is chronophotographic: multiple positional states of the same motion collapsed into one object, readable as both sequence and form simultaneously. — Barkitecture CoWork Briefing, March 2026

Precedent: Étienne-Jules Marey (1880s). Marey's chronophotographs collapse multiple temporal states of a moving subject into a single image. The form that emerges is the envelope of all positions — not any single one. Here, that logic is literalized: a concrete module encodes the full kinematic range of the behavior it celebrates.

The geometry is not illustrative — it is derived. Every dimensional parameter (asymmetry, arc, depth, lean) traces directly to peer-reviewed kinematic data. The module's form is the argument's evidence.

Data Pipeline

Raw data: comma-delimited x/y/z.txt files per dog per session. Rows are individual cycles; 76 values per row. Filtered to exactly 76-value rows → 19,937 valid cycles.

# Step 1 — Load X = np.array([row for dog in dogs for row in load_rows(dog,'x') if len(row)==76]) # (19937, 76) # Step 2 — Core derivations mean_x = np.mean(X, axis=0) # (76,) mean lateral profile dX = np.gradient(mean_x) # angular velocity dwelling_norm = 1 / np.abs(dX) # dwelling time dwelling_norm /= dwelling_norm.max() # normalize [0, 1] # Step 3 — Percentile arrays (X-direction spread across all cycles) p5x = np.percentile(X, 5, axis=0) # (76,) lower extreme p25x = np.percentile(X, 25, axis=0) # (76,) lower quartile p75x = np.percentile(X, 75, axis=0) # (76,) upper quartile p95x = np.percentile(X, 95, axis=0) # (76,) upper extreme L_APEX = np.argmin(mean_x) # = 27
